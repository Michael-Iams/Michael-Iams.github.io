    In the name of God Amen. I William Iiams of Alegany County and State of
    Maryland being in perfect helth of body and of sound and disposing mind
    memory and understanding considering the certainty of death and uncertainty
    of the time thereof and being desirous to settle my worldly affairs and
    thereby be the better prepared to leave this world when it shall please god
    to call me hence. Do therefore make and publish this my last will and
    testament in manner and form following.
    That is to say - first and principally I commit my soul into the hands of
    almightly god and my body to the earth to be decently buried at the
    discretion of my executors hereafter named. I devise and bequeath as
    follows:
    Item 1 I do hereby constitute and appoint my eldest son Amos and Jesse my
    youngest son as joint heirs and executors of all my real and personal estate
    except that track of land which I bought of Thomas Boein-----
    Item 2 I give and devise to my oldest and youngest sons Amos and Jesse the
    plantation or farm on which I now live containing one track of land called
    Williams farm containing two hundred and forty six acres and one quarter of
    an acre and one called Addition toWilliams farm containing 200 1/2 of an acre
    and one track called Partnership containing 60 acres, thirty of its mine &
    thirty (S)? Danisons all which joins each other in the county and state
    aforesaid and one track which I bought of Thomas Moghon lying on town creek
    in Bedford County, Pennsylvania them their heirs and assigns - - - - -
    Item 3 I give and devise to my second son William my land on which he now
    lives lying in Bedford County and state of Pennsylvania to him the said
    William his heirs and assigns.
    Item 4 Amos and Jesse shall pay Mary Gorden eighty Dollars it being a
    ballance due her or her heirs of her legacy.
    Lastly I do hereby constitute and appoint my two sons Amos and Jesse as
    joint heirs of my personal property and what they shall pay my daughter
    Rachel or her heirs the sum of one hundred and thirty dollars and Rebecca or
    her heirs the sum of one hundred and thirty dollars and they shall appoint
    too appraisers that are uninterested to appraise the property and Mary,
    Rachel and Rebecca should take property at the appraisement or take ___due
    notes to the amount of half there dowry and I do certify that Isbel Sloan
    shall have free liberty on my place as usual in and to both the dwelling
    house & C house both her and her children for the term of three months
    after my death and she Isbel Sloa ,Amos and Jesse shall give in all my
    personal property to the best of their knowledge except what the appraisers
    shall ____ sufficient of both meat and bread for the term of three months for
    the said Isbel and her two children ratifying and confirming this to be my
    last will and testament .
    W Iiams (his seal) signed sealed and declared by W. Iiams.
    The above named testator as and for his last will and testament in the
    presence of us
    Who at his request in his prsence and the presence of each other) have
    subscribed our names as witnesses thereto this twenty second day of June in
    the year of our Lord one thousand eight hundred and twenty-four.

    Moses G. Robinett
    Isaac Sloan

    There is no occation or need of any Legal administration . . . . (rest of
    will unreadable)

    From:
    Subject: Iiams Family Origins
    Date: Wed, 21 Apr 1999 11:02:02 EDT

    Hi, Iiams researchers!

    I'm writing in response to the posting by Michael Dorsey Iams in which he
    says:

    "It seems that all Iams/Ijams/Imes in the United States descend from a
    William Iiams who immigrated to Anne Arundel County, Maryland around 1660.
    It is unclear where he came from, but it seems most likely came from
    Wales, possibly as a result of the English Civil War between the Oliver
    Cromwell led Parliment and King Charles I. The first record of him is his
    marriage to Elizabeth Cheyney in 1669. That marriage produced 6 children
    from which
    all of us descend."

    Of course, the part about William Iiams' origins in Wales are highly
    speculative, and we ought to be careful in passing on that information (lest
    it become "fact") unless we always preface it as Michael did.

    This is not the first time that I have seen it stated that ALL the Iiams
    (variant spellings) in the United States are descended from William Iiams and
    Elizabeth Cheyney. To make this assertion, you first have to know EVERY
    Iiams in the United States and their ancestry. The statement implies that no
    other member of this, or any other Iiams family ever immigrated to the U.S.
    and bore descendants; an assertion, I think, that nobody is qualified to make.

    I am descended from William Iams who lived in Bedford County, PA and Allegany
    County, MD and died in the latter in 1831. His will in Allegany Co., MD was
    also copied some years later, into the Bedford County, PA Court Records,
    presumably for reasons of land transfer; some of the property in his will
    being in that county.

    Many of his descendants, including myself and earlier generations who still
    live on and near the old Bedford County property have tried, in vain, to make
    a connection to the Anne Arundel, MD, Iiams family. We would all dearly love
    to find a connection to this venerable family. In my own research, I have
    come tantalizingly close at times, thinking I was about to solve this
    mystery, only in the end to fail to find necessary documentation.

    All that aside, I have written some "alternate" theories as to the origin of
    my William Iams who died in 1831 in Allegany Co., PA which I will send to the
    newsgroup in a seperate posting. However, I think I have adequate
    documentation to disprove the statement that all Iiams in the U.S descend
    from William Iiams and Elizabeth Cheyney.

    The earliest Iams known to have been in Bedford County, PA was one Richard
    Iams (occasional differing spellings). He is mentioned in some early
    histories of Bedford County as being one of twelve or thirteen settlers who
    settled in what came to be known as the "Spurgeon Settlement"; one of the
    first white settlements in the county. Richard's grave marker (the second
    oldest known in the county) read, "Jan 26th 1758, in his 72nd year". And
    here is the crux of my point. That means that Richard was born in 1686.

    RICHARD IAMS OF BEDFORD COUNTY, PA 1686 - 1758:

    is clearly not the Richard Iiams who was the son of William Iiams and
    Elizabeth Cheyney, the Anne Arundel, MD immigrants. Their son, Richard was
    born about 1673 and died in Anne Arundel County, MD in 1761.

    William Iiams and Elizabeth Cheyney's oldest (known) child was William.
    Being born in 1670, this William, Jr. COULD have fathered a child named
    Richard when he was sixteen, but this is almost certainly not the case both
    because of its unliklihood and the fact that this William, Jr. had a son
    named Richard who was born in 1702. William, Jr.'s (married Elizabeth
    Plummer) first (known) child was Elizabeth, born in 1697.

    My conclusion is that Richard Iams of Bedford County COULD NOT BE a
    descendant of William Iiams and Elizabeth Cheyney. He could have been old
    William's brother, but we have no indication of this. He could have been
    another relative, or he could have been unrelated. Wouldn't we like to know?

    My relatives and I continue to seek any information leading to the discovery
    of the parents, spouse, and family of William Iams (prob. born before 1755)
    who lived in Bedford County, PA and died, with will, in Allegany County, MD,
    in 1831.

    Christopher Bryce
    Greenville, South Carolina

    From:
    Subject: William Iams of Allegany Co., MD and Bedford Co., PA
    Date: Wed, 21 Apr 1999 11:02:10 EDT

    Here is my theory of the origin of my ancestor, William Iams, who lived in
    Bedford County, PA and also Allegany County, MD where he died in 1831.

    William lived on land in Bedford Co., PA that is known as "Spurgeon's
    Choice", part of the very early Spurgeon Settlement. How William Iams got
    Spurgeon's Choice land is not known. There is apparently no known deed of
    transfer.

    Many researchers have ignored something that I am beginning to see as
    significant. We know that there was a Richard Iames who settled in or near
    Spurgeon's Choice and died there on 26 Jan 1758. He was 72 years old, which
    means he was born about 1686. He doesn't fit with any known Iiams of the old
    Eastern Maryland Iiams family about which Newman wrote so much. Of that
    family, he is too old to be any of the immigrant William and Elizabeth's
    children, nor do we have any information about the immigrant William having
    any brother or other relatives that came over. We simply cannot tell who he
    was, where he came from (although the other settlers at Spurgeon's Choice are
    said to have come from Virginia - and this could have been what is now West
    Virginia and may not have been very distant from the Maryland settlement) or
    if there he was any relation to the more famous Maryland family mentioned.

    The fact is, we find our Iams family and some of its descendants down to
    today living right on the Spurgeon's Choice land that this old Richard lived
    on, and his gravestone is (or was, in recent and documented memory) still on
    the property. That, in itself, is a pretty strong argument for our descent
    from this Richard, and it has been almost completely ignored; perhaps by the
    desire to link up with the Eastern Maryland family and the fact that this
    Richard just doesn't fit in. I think we'd be further ahead if we just
    ignored the Anne Arundel, MD family unless our research can establish a link
    by working backwards in time, one step at a time. When you can't find who an
    ancestors parents were, the line simply ends, and there's not much you can do
    about it. Again, trying to establish a connection with the Eastern Maryland
    family has received hundreds and hundreds of hours of research by many Iams
    descendants including myself, and we are no closer to establishing a
    connection with this family that when we began, so far as I can tell.

    My theory, then, has become that Richard is our ancestor. I'd be very
    willing to see this theory disproved and change my findings accordingly. But
    how can we ignore the historical record that he was one of the first settlers
    in the area, and that the Iames family has been seen to have lived on this
    same land for nearly three hundred years? That's not proof, but it is darn
    strong evidence.

    We know nothing of this early Richard's children. The first courthouse
    record we have of Iamses in Bedford County is a William, who appears on the
    tax lists in 1783. He is listed with sons who were older than the known sons
    of our William, who died in Allegany Co., MD in 1831, and appears almost
    certainly to be a different person. He is the one I think is our William
    1831s father, and I conjecture, also the father of Hugh, who shows up in
    Bedford Co., PA's tax records. Hugh Iiams appears on the tax lists for the
    first time in 1789, perhaps when he reached the age of majority (and I'm not
    sure what that age was; 16 - 21, I imagine - 18 would be a good ballpark
    figure to use for now). In 1798, William Iiams, Sr. granted to Hugh Iiams a
    parcel of land in Spurgeon's Choice, lying on Town Creek (to begin to answer
    my own question). Most likely, Hugh was granted this land from his father.
    He could have bought, or otherwise received it from his supposed brother,
    William 1831, but this William was probably his younger brother, and purchase
    from his father seems much more likely.

    In 1808, a William Iiams, Sr. and a William Iiams, Jr. appear together as
    witnesses to a will. We can't prove much by it; our William 1831 had a son,
    William, who would have been about 22 that year. Could be them; could be the
    older William d.1831 and his father. Also, in those days, "Jr." and "Sr."
    did not necessarily mean father and son. "Sr." was sometimes used to
    differentiate between two related people of the same name; not necessarily
    father and son.

    We can now conjecture that William, Sr., was the father of Hugh and William
    d. 1831, got the land from his father, and his father may have been the early
    pioneer settler, Richard Iames. I am now piling conjecture upon unproven
    fact, but I think this is a step ahead of what we have produced before.
    Ulitmately, we can't prove my theory, because the early records of these
    things, to my knowledge, don't exist.

    My relatives and I continue to seek any information leading to the discovery
    of the parents, spouse, and family of William Iams (prob. born before 1755)
    who lived in Bedford County, PA and died, with will, in Allegany County, MD,
    in 1831. Many thanks to all researchers on this problem.

    Christopher Bryce
    Greenville, South Carolina
