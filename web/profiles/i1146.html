Published: June 19, 1987

Margaret Seton Ijams, a former vice president of the Community Service Society, New York's oldest family welfare organization, died Tuesday in her Manhattan apartment. She was 92 years old and also lived in Lawrence, L.I.

Mrs. Ijams was a collateral descendant of Mother Elizabeth Bayley Seton, the first native-born American to become a saint. In 1917, she joined the board of the Virginia Day Nursery, which her mother helped found in 1879, and remained on the board until her death. She was also active on the boards of the Community Service Society and the Federation of Protestant Welfare Agencies.

She is survived by three sons, Seton, of Bowdoinham, Me., Maitland, of Barneveld, N.Y., and Porter, of Locust Valley, L.I.; nine grandchildren, and one great grandchild.

September 29, 1975 Vol. 4 No. 13 Twenty-eight of Mother Seton's American Relatives Witness Her Proclaimed a Saint in RomeBy Logan Bentley

By any kind of reckoning, they were a curious platoon: a French countess, two WASP octogenarians, a British nun, the wife of an American admiral, the daughter of a Harvard paleontologist, and an aide to Eisenhower.

But they all had one trait in common. They could count themselves among the handful of living relatives of Mother Elizabeth Ann Seton, the first native American saint.

Some 28 American relatives of Mother Seton were among the 15,000 pilgrims from the United States gathered in Rome last week to witness Pope Paul VI proclaim that "We declare and define that Blessed Elizabeth Ann Bayley Seton is a saint." Mother Seton—an Episcopalian who converted to Catholicism and founded the first U.S. parochial school and the first U.S. religious order—had five children of her own. But her last direct descendant died in 1967. There are, however, three main lines of descendants through her uncle, her husband's brother and her half-brothers and sisters.

The largest family contingent in Rome were the Shanleys, 27 of them, led by Bernard M. Shanley, a partner in one of the largest law firms in New Jersey and a great-great-grand-nephew of Mother Seton. A Roman Catholic, Shanley was one of the campaign managers who helped steer Eisenhower to the White House, and served as deputy to Chief of Staff Sherman Adams. "President Eisenhower once told me that the greatest contribution ever made in this country was the parochial school system," he says proudly.

George Sommaripa, a bearded part-time politician and real estate agent, lives in Cambridge, Mass. on a small income "from a slice of the Lorillard fortune." His grandmother was a member of the millionaire tobacco clan. A 1962 convert to the Russian rite of Catholicism, he brought his Jewish wife and their small son to the ceremonies. Of his famous ancestress he says, "When I am an old man, I want to say I was here in Rome. And that's why I brought my little boy to see it."

Among the other interested spectators were Helen Seton de Goyon, who went to school in New York, married a Frenchman and became the Comtesse D'August, and Mrs. C.S. Williams Jr., wife of a rear admiral with the Mediterranean fleet.

Among the youngest of the group in Rome was a student at New Trier High School in Winnetka, Ill., Elizabeth (Lisa) Bayley McMahan. She is the great-great-great-grandniece of Mother Seton. She is Catholic and bears a close resemblance to her saintly relative. "I heard about her all my life, but I never imagined they would make her a saint," says Lisa. "It was unreal."

Sister Anne Bayley, a mother superior of the Company of Mary, an English teaching order, did not know of her relationship to Mother Seton until 1963. Says she, "When the Holy Father kept repeating her name, which is also mine, I had this feeling of closeness, of affinity."

The Ijams family, led by Mrs. J. Horton Ijams, 80, her three sons, Maitland, Seton and Porter, two daughters-in-law, and her older sister, Mrs. Harold Pardee, 84, came to Rome as the result of a joke. Like Mother Seton before her conversion, they are wealthy, social New Yorkers. Seton works for a distiller, Maitland is a producer at a summer theater, and Porter ("I'm practically an atheist") is the world's 13th-ranked backgammon player.

All of the Ijams are Episcopalian except for Seton's wife, Nancy, a Catholic. When she announced that she wanted to go to Rome, her husband said he thought the whole family should go to witness the canonization of their saintly relative. "That's fine," said the dowager Mrs. Ijams. "Who's going to pay for it?" "You are, Mother," Seton replied, and she agreed, "provided you all pay your own bar bills." "Mother knew what she was talking about," said Porter. "She knew the bar bills would cost more than the trip."
