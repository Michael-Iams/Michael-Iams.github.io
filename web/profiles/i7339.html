Obituary
By Debbie Kushlan For the Idaho Press-Tribune (Sept 1, 2013)
http://www.idahopress.com/members/dollie-iiams-slowing-down-after-an-active-career/article_00a6b3c4-12b7-11e3-a183-0019bb2963f4.html?mode=story

In her lifetime, Dollie Iiams, 84, has driven pilot cars, worked in cafes and helped teachers after school. Now, she has slowed down and enjoys spending time at the Parma Senior Center. She has been a regular at the senior center since 1982. She goes there for lunch almost every weekday.

When asked if she has made a lot of friends at the senior center, Iiams said, “Let’s hope so. I don’t think I have any enemies!”

Senior center coordinator Fran Branham confirmed that Iiams is a wonderful lady and everyone loves her.

Besides lunch, the senior center offers knitting and crocheting every Thursday. Iiams prefers to crochet. She especially enjoys the weekly musical performance by a group of musicians from Emmett. On guitar and piano, they play a variety of country music.
“Oh, that’s such pretty music,” Iiams said.

Iiams was born in Nebraska and moved with her mother and two brothers to Parma in the mid-1940s at age 14. She attended school in Parma. Medical issues caused her to miss much of her eleventh year and she never went back to school. Instead, she got married and started a family.

Family is very important to Iiams. Her three children still live in the area. Son Vernon Booth and his wife Jackie have lived with Iiams since 2011, when they moved in to help care for her after she fell and broke her hip.

"“It’s company for me,” she said. “I enjoy having them around.”

Daughter Carol Ann Atagi lives in Nyssa and son George Booth is in Parma. Iiams has six grandchildren, six great-grandchildren and six great-great-grandchildren. Her husband Chet Iiams passed away in 1998.

“One morning I woke up and realized all of my eight brothers and sisters were gone but me, and it sure felt lonesome.”

During high school, Iiams stayed after school and worked for the teachers, helping with housework and cleaning. She also worked for three different cafes through the years, including 13 years at the B & B Café in Parma, “just to fill up my time.”

Most recently, Iiams retired from a long career driving pilot cars in Idaho as well as Montana, Nebraska and other places.

“It was fun and they were nice to me,” Iiams said. “I worked for one guy 34 years, maybe two or three other guys during the week. It was kind of interesting. We’d work daylight to dark usually. Get up in morning, work all day, go home and fall into bed, then get up in the morning and do it all again.”

When she was younger, she sewed a lot. She made clothes for herself, her daughter and even for customers.

“I made quite a bit of money sewing for other people,” Iiams said.

She doesn’t sew or crochet anymore, but she does do a lot of reading.  She’s always looking for good books to read.

Even though Iiams’ dog, Rex, was the reason she broke her hip (he got excited and wrapped his chain around her legs, causing her to fall) she still loved him. Now she has Remington by her side.

“He’s just a mutt, but he’s great company,” she said.
