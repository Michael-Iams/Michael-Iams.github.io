    Note #1

    Mary Iiams b. Ma6 6, 1705, d/o of William Iiams and Elizabeth Plummer
    married John Waters b. May 30, 1696 s/o of John Waters & Elizabeth Giles.
    They are quite a few children one of which was Mary Waters b. abt 1726 and
    married Edmond [Edmund] Jennings [Jennings]. Mary Waters 1726 and Edmond
    Jennings dob? had a number of children 4 girls and two boys. Edmund
    Jennings, Jr. & Peter Thomas Jennings. Peter married Jemima Waters d/o of
    William Waters & Rachel Jones. Peter & Jemima had two sons Thomas & Daniel.
    [Peter & Jemima were first cousins].
    Sarah Ijams b. aft 1720 d/o of Richard Iiams & Elizabeth Gaither was
    not married to Benjamin Gaither b. 1712 s/o of John Gaither & Jane Buck.
    Sarah & John Gaither had two children: Benjamin Ijams and Lloyd Iiams.
    Later, Sarah married a William Peach and they had a son William Peach, Jr.
    who was the half-brother of Benjamin.
    The Jenings family and its connections with many personages of our
    nation's history might have exercised more influence that it seems to have
    done. The first to arrive in America was commonly called Col. Edward Jenings.
    He was the son of Sir Edmund, member of Parliament, and Margaret [Barham]
    Jenings, daughter of a lord mayor of London. About 1680 he removed to
    Virginia where he acquired vast land holdings. He assisted in the
    establishment of the College of William & Mary and of Bruton Parish Church in
    Williamsburg, where he is buried. He was a member of the King's Council for
    over 30 years and served as a governor of the colony for a short period of
    time. Among his descendants were Edmund Randolph and Robert E. Lee
    His son, Edmund Jenings, Esq., became secretary of the province of
    Maryland and a substantial landowner there. One of his properties was a
    plantation of 600 acres [probably Waters lot, Robin Hood Forrest] situated at
    the forks of the Patuxent River in Anne Arundel County. It was managed by
    his nephew, Edmund Jenings, Sr., father of Edmund Jr. and Peter. Extensive
    research on the part of several descendants has failed to discover
    information about the other son of Col. Jenings, who would have been Edmund,
    Sr.'s father. Although proof is lacking, the evidence suggests his name was
    William.
    A "Letterbook of Edmund Jenings [1703-1765] and Edmund Jenings
    [1731-1819]," containing correspondence of Edmund, Esq. and his son, whom we
    shall simply call Edmund, is of interest in this narrative. Edmund Jenings,
    Esq. had returned to England where he died in 1756. By the terms of his will,
    Edmund, Sr. inherited most of the personal property under his care and the
    use of the plantation but "only for his life." All the land in America was
    devised to the testator's son, Edmund. Though born and reared in Maryland,
    this Edmund always maintained his principal residence in London during his
    adult life. From there wrote to his cousins, Edmund Sr., to wish him "joy of
    what my father has left to you and assure you may depend upon everything that
    our dear connection and friendship can require of me."
    The letters show that Edmund, Sr. was not a particularly good steward of
    his responsibilities in his uncle's lifetime, and the situation grew worse
    under the new ownership. He not only refused to pay the overdue rent and
    bills owned to the estate but claimed his uncle had promised him the
    plantation. However, even though Edmund of London did not acknowledge that
    contention, it is probably that Edmund, Sr. continued to live there until his
    death in 1791.
    During the War of Independence, it happened in Maryland, if not in other
    of the colonies, that land of British subjects was confiscated for the use of
    the government. In 1790, Edmund Jenings, Sr. had informed the authorities of
    the status of the property he occupied; several years later his son, Peter,
    brought suit to force the sale of the land and recover to himself one-third
    of its value as his reward for calling it to their attention. The suit was
    denied. Edmund Jenings, it seems, had been in a position to benefit American
    prisoners in England during the war, and a grateful government declined to
    enforce the statute. It was shortly thereafter that Peter appeared in the
    record of events that led to his execution.
    In the Maryland Gazette for 23 August 1798, Governor John Henry issued a
    proclamation for the apprehension of anyone known to be responsible for the
    burning of the storehouse of Robert Magill and the tobacco house of Joseph
    Duvall in the month of July in Prince George's County. Prior to that Peter
    Jennings and his companions were already in a heap of trouble. In April he
    and Richard Waters had been fined costs of 4800 pounds of tobacco with 32
    pounds added for being tardy [cause not specified]. During the next month
    Sutton Isaac, Benjamin Ijams, and William Peach appeared in court on charges
    of assault and battery on a black man. Benjamin Ijams was also tried for
    striking Joseph Duvall with a horse whip. On 1 June, Peter Jenings and Basil
    Evans appears for assaulting and beating Milley Gray.
    Richard Waters and Benjamin Ijams were close relatives of Peter, and
    William Peach was one of the witnesses to his will. Criminal court records
    for the month of September show further appearances after Peter's execution:
    Benjamin Ijams for setting fire to and burning the storehouse of Edward
    Gaither; William Peach for receiving from Peter Jenings a large sum of money
    in bank notes that he knew was stolen; Negro Dick, slave to Thomas Jones
    Waters, for receiving from Benjamin Ijams money and goods from Peter Jenings,
    LLoyd Ijams, and Benjamin Ijams that he knew was stolen from Robert Magill's
    storehouse, and for aiding Peter Jenings and Lloyd Ijams in plundering and
    burning the building
    Peter Jenings was hanged for the crime of arson. With our limited
    knowledge of facts, one can only speculate as to the motivation for his
    outrageous behavior. To say it was the result of rancor toward his wealthy
    English cousin may be wide of the mark. Perhaps its roots lie within the
    unknown history of his grandfather, William, for Peter's father was
    profligate as well. Whether the reprieve was issued because the punishment
    was judged too severe goes unstated.
    Last Will & Testament written on the day of his execution:
    In his will he desired that his property be sold in order to pay his
    debts and make restitution. 'likewise to them I have injured which I lieve on
    a piece of papers signed by the same witness." And though he persisted to
    the bitter end in the struggle with Edmund Jenings, his last thoughts were
    for his family. The instructions to his executor:
    My desire is he Shall take possession of the Old Plantation where my
    Farther live--the write of information is surely due my Farther Family which
    I desire they shall receive my Brother to take possession of it - - after my
    Brother Sells all the property in his power after a payment made of all my
    just debts and to the Persons I have injured - - what ever left if any - -
    the money to be plaid on Interest every year the Persons who have my Children
    to receive each of them one half of the Interest of the Money for their
    support when they come to twenty one years of age they to be paid their
    parts. I appoint my brother above mentioned, Edmund Jinings whole and Sole
    Executor and a Guardian for my two little Children Daniel & Thomas Jinings
    given under my hand & seal this Seventh day of September 1798. Peter
    Jinings..[Maryland Genealogical Society Bulletin, Vol 30, No. 3 Summer 1989,
    Peter Jenings, Firebrand by Arliss S. Monk]
