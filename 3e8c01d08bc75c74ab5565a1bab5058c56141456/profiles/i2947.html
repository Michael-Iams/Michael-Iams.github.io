    Sports Illustrated - Letter to Editor July 10, 1967

    Sirs:
    Never before have I read an article in your magazine as prejudiced as Alfred Wright's article on Jack Nicklaus' U.S. Open victory (Jack Delivers the Crusher, June 26). I am one of the first to admit that Jack Nicklaus is one of the finest golfers in the world. But let's be fair, shall we? Several of Mr. Wright's comments about Arnold Palmer were cruel and unnecessary. Loyalty and admiration for individual athletes are fine until one loses sight of the qualities and talents of other athletes, too. In my opinion, this is precisely what Mr. Wright has done.
    CHARLES GARY IAMS
    Marion, Ohio

    A real music man
    Gary Iams is a legend in his own right. He is a retired music educator and is currently serving his eighth year on the Marion City Board of Education — the same school system from where he retired.

    One of the stories he loves to tell is how his parents met in high school. Both were in the Acappella Choir directed by L.G. Jones when they met. Jones took the Acappela Choir several places to sing to raise money for war bonds during World War II.

    He believes his parents passed their own love of music and the arts on to him.

    In 1970, while a student at Bowling Green State University, he was invited by Jones to play the cello for the annual presentation of Messiah by the Marion Civic Chorus.

    “I did that a long time,’ Iams said, adding that in the mid-1980s, he became a member of the chorus board and changed from playing the cello to singing. In 1989, he became the director. The Dec. 2 performance this year will mark his 25th year as director.

    George Lane was director of the Marion Harding High School Chamber Orchestra when Iams played in it, he said.

    Iams retired in 2002 and said as he left he knew the program remained in good hands.

    He has attended the Capture the Moment event before and is excited and honored to be a part of it this year.

    “I think it’s amazing what those folks (original committee) have done with this event,” he said. “What better way to preserve our history than to have the people who have experienced it share it. I’m proud to be a part of it.”

    Thurston stated the event is an opportunity for old friends to gather and hear stories related by the panelists.

    The location is handicap accessible and offers plenty of parking. Thurston noted the dinner is different this year as it will be a served dinner.

    “This is going to be great and we appreciate the generosity of Primrose to donate and serve the food,” Thurston said. “They are a good friend of the community.”
