    Note #1

    HUDSON - For three generations, the Ijams of Hudson and Atlanta have been doing their best to do their duty to God and country.
    On Tuesday, Jake Ijams, 14, of Troop 57 in Hudson, joined brother, Luke, 16; father, Kirk, a Normal police lieutenant; and grandfather, Allyn, in the ranks of Boy Scouting's elite.
    In an age when wholesome activities like camping with dad are overshadowed by headlines of gang violence and drug abuse, Jake Ijams became an Eagle Scout.
    His late grandfather achieved scouting's highest rank in the late 1940s. His father made the grade in 1979. Luke earned his Eagle in 2000.
    "Even though there are things out there to entice kids to do wrong, scouting stands by its values," said Kirk Ijams, explaining why scouting continues to attract 1 million boys each year. About 43,000 became Eagle Scouts in 2001.
    "Parents nowadays are getting more involved with their children. This is one place to do that," he said.
    The Boy Scouts of America's national headquarters has no statistics on how rare it is to have three generations of Eagle Scouts in one family. But only about 4 percent of all scouts earn Eagle status. Just 1 million Eagle Scout Awards have been given since the scouts were organized in 1910.
    The feat requires earning 21 merit badges on various topics and completing a service project to benefit the community, an organization or a church.
    Jake built and distributed wood-duck houses in conjunction with the Green Wings program of Ducks Unlimited. Luke coordinated the painting of curbs yellow to highlight the location of fire plugs in Hudson.
    If scouting had started in the U.S. earlier, the string of Eagle Scouts in the Ijams family could have been even longer. Jake and Luke's great-grandfather, the late Dr. R. L. Ijams of Atlanta, became active as an adult and earned the prestigious Silver Beaver Award for establishing Boy Scouting in Atlanta. That's where Allyn and Kirk Ijams earned their Eagle awards with Allyn later earning the Silver Beaver Award, too.
    Kirk Ijams is carrying on that tradition of service as troop committee chairman.
    Outdoor skills are scouting's foundation, and the Hudson troop, chartered through the Hudson Lion's Club, camps every month. At the end of June, half of the 35-member troop heads to a scout camp in Canada. Younger campers will spend that same week at a camp near Peoria.
    The troop helps with community functions, too, such as last Saturday's Fishing Derby at Bloomington's Miller Park. And, it will host its annual Pancake and Sausage Breakfast from 7 to 11 a.m. Sunday - Father's Day - at the Hudson Town Hall.
    Kirk Ijams quoted research that shows of every 100 scouts, 12 develop hobbies that last a lifetime. Eight enter careers based on interests born while working for a merit badge. One will save someone's life with skills learned in scouting. Twelve get their first exposure to religion. One joins the clergy.
    And, four become Eagles.
