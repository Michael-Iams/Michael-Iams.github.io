    Posted: Wednesday, August 12, 2015 12:00 am
    By Danny Haralson columnist 
    There are those around us who always seem to think they know what is best for you and me and involve themselves in seeing that it is so. People like this think that only their way is the right way, and heaven help us when two or more of them get together with the same agenda.
    An example of this getting out of control occurred many years ago when women were enjoying newly won freedoms in voting and prohibition. They felt they needed to be involved in curbing the sins they believed inflicted most men, which included drinking and gambling.
    In the early evening hours of Sept. 19, 1921, Mrs. J. A. McNeil, Bessie H. Cooper and Daisy McCollum, all of whom were of mid-age, were strolling down Central Avenue in Safford when they passed by the Olive Hotel. They observed what they thought was a card gambling game going on in one of the upstairs rooms of the hotel.
    Since they were at ground level and would have had to stand atop an automobile to see the table and cards, it is unknown how they came to the conclusion that an illegal card game was taking place, but they did. They immediately reported what they saw to County Attorney E. L. Spriggs, who, failing to find the sheriff, called Town Marshal Jacobson, who made the arrest onsite of Ab Stevens, son of our first sheriff and a deputy sheriff himself; Henry Clifford, Wilbur Ijams, who was one of our prominent businessmen; Jim Anderson and a Russell Wines.
    Also staying at the hotel and in the room at the time were Messrs. Horn, Webster and Tanner. They were requested to appear before Justice Bilby for trial the following day.
    The men appeared at the courthouse Wednesday afternoon before Judge Bilby and asked for a change of venue, which was granted, and the case was taken to Thatcher, where it was tried before Justice D. D. Phillips. Six jurors were chosen, and the prosecution called on Marshal Jacobson, Mrs. Cooper, Mrs. McNeil and Miss McCollum to testify. Ab Stevens and Mr. Horn were the witnesses for the defense.
    After the ladies gave their testimony as to what they saw, they were asked if they could see any cards, liquor, money or even the card table from their vantage point at ground level in front of the hotel, and they replied no, but that they knew what was going on anyway. Marshal Jacobson testified that when he entered the room, the men were playing cards, but no money was on the table and none in sight, and no alcohol was present.
    The jury, after deliberating three hours, brought in a verdict of not guilty and the men were discharged but, because of the notoriety caused by the case, the district attorney was asked about how far the gambling laws could be carried, and his response was that women playing cards for a prize at a clubhouse are as guilty of gambling as men in a stud poker game for money or drinks. He was asked if the law would be applicable to card playing in incorporated private clubs, and he said, “Assuredly it would.”
    So because of the actions of busybodies, new attention would fall onto the women of the community as well while drinking their tea and eating their cookies, if engaged in card playing and if any gain was to be realized in any way.
    Of course, this was only a temporary setback to the busy-bodies of our town and the damage they could do and, truth be told, the men in the Olive Hotel that night were gambling.
