    Gassaway Watkins (1752-1840)
    MSA SC 3520-16779

    Biography: Born 1752 in Anne Arundel County, Maryland;
    Son of Nicholas Watkins Jr. and Ariana Worthington.
    Siblings: Margaret, Thomas, Elizabeth, John, Nicholas.
    Married Sarah Jones.
    Married Ruth Dorsey.
    Children: Gassaway Jr., Bonaparte, Thomas, Turenne, Charlotte, Ann Elizabeth, Harriet (b. 1792).
    Married Eleanora Clagett.
    Children: Caroline (1804-1896), Camadel (b. 1805), Eleanor (b. 1807), Amanda (b. 1809), William (b. 1810), John (b. 1813), Elizabeth (b. 1815), Priscilla (1817-1893), Margaret (1819-1896), Albina (1822-1899). Died July 14th, 1840.

    According to the Archives of Maryland Online Gassaway Watkins (listed as "Gazaway") enlisted as a corporal in Captain Patrick Sim's Second Company on January 29th, 1776. However, the Historical Register of Officers of the Continental Army states that Watkins enlisted as a Sergeant on January 14th, 1776. His pension also records him as enlisting as a Sergeant. In any case Watkins participated in the Battles of Long Island (August 27th) and White Plains (October 28th) as a Sergeant.

    In November Watkins fell sick and his company left him in Morristown, New Jersey. Despite his sickness, Watkins was promoted to Ensign on December 10th. Remaining ill, Watkins made the journey without clothing or money from Morristown back to Annapolis. Suffering from smallpox Watkins confined himself to his room. He received the smallpox inoculation in April 1777 and on the 17th of that month was transferred to the Seventh Maryland Regiment as a 2nd Lieutenant. Watkins rejoined the army shortly before the Battle of Germantown (October 4th, 1777). He spent the winter at Wilmington and saw combat again in a light infantry unit at the Battle of Monmouth (June 28th 1778). After the battle he camped in Bownbrook, where he rose to the rank of 1st Lieutenant on September 14th. He went on furlough in December and rejoined in April 1779.

    Lieutenant Watkins was present for both the American defeat at the Battle of Camden (August 16th, 1780) and for the decisive American victory at the Battle of Cowpens (January 17th, 1781). In addition, Watkins would go on to participate in the Battles of Guilford, Ninety-Six, and Eutaw. He was promoted to Captain on May 13th, 1782. He left the service in April of 1783.

    Upon the conclusion of the war, Watkins married Sarah Jones, daughter of Captain Isaac Jones. She died within one year, bearing him no children. His second wife was Ruth Dorsey, daughter of Captain John Dorsey. In 1785 Watkins built his second wife a home in Anne Arundel County (now Howard), naming it "Walnut Grove." Watkins and his wife had several children together before she died at an unknown date. He married again on April 26th, 1803 to Eleanor Bowie Clagett (1783-1871), who bore him eight daughters and two sons. Finding himself with financial difficulties due to his large household, Watkins petitioned the Maryland House of Delegates for an annuity that could give him some relief. In November of 1807 his petition was approved and he began to receive half pay of the amount a Captain would receive. He later applied for a military pension and began receiving 20 dollars per month on April 23rd, 1818. He reapplied for his pension in 1828, having not received any funds since 1820. Gassaway Watkins died on July 14th, 1840.

    Watkin's widow reapplied for his pension and began receiving 480 dollars per year on February 3rd, 1853.

    Gassaway Watkins' role in the Battle of Long Island earns him a place among the Maryland 400.
