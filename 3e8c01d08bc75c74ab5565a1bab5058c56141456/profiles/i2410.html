    This column is about University of Tennessee football, but if you're not a football fan, please keep reading anyway. It's not about the game or won-loss records. It's about a few unusual facts concerning UT's early football history.
    For instance, who today remembers that the quarterback on the first UT team 110 years ago was Howard A.Ijams, a member of the family whose home place in South Knoxville became Ijams Nature Park? He became a doctor and practiced here.
    Ijams was small -- 5 feet, 110 pounds. News reports said he was "one of the best quarterbacks in the South" and that he also "was a skilled performer on the horizontal bar, but it was as a tumbler that he achieved astonishing success."
    He remained remarkably agile all his life. When he was in his late 40s, he would amaze his friends by turning backward summersaults from a standing position, "a feat that few younger men attempt," news reports said.
    "Merely removing his coat, Dr. Ijams would perform the feat, making a perfect graceful landing on his feet with the ease of a professional."
    Ijams was killed in a traffic accident on Magnolia Avenue in 1923 at the age of 49 when his car was hit by a streetcar.
    The Journal and Tribune newspaper bannered the story in inch-high letters on page one. Ijams was the brother of Harry P. Ijams, owner of the Ijams Nature Park site.
    Ijams and his teammates played UT's first game before about 100 fans at Chattanooga on Nov. 21, 1891, a Saturday, according to former Knoxville Journal sports editor Russ Bebb in his book "The Big Orange." It was the only game played that year. UT lost to Sewanee 24- 0.
    News reports about the outcome didn't appear in the Knoxville press until four days later, Bebb wrote.
    The courses of study of Ijams and his teammates were Latin, Greek, science, chemistry, civil engineering, mechanical engineering and law, according to Bebb.
    During the early years, the UT teams practiced and played games on the same field, Wait Field, on the southeast corner of Cumberland Avenue and 15th Street (known today as Phillip Fulmer Way). UT's tennis courts were later developed on that site and remained there for years.
    The team moved to the new Shields-Watkins Field in 1921, playing the first football game there 80 years ago this weekend. The new field was first used for a baseball game in the spring of that year.
    During the early 1900s, two UT head coaches left to become high school head coaches, John Bender at Knoxville High School in 1920 and M.B. Banks at Knox County's Central High School in 1925.
    Banks was succeeded at UT by one of his assistants, the coach who stayed 17 years and became legend, Robert R. Neyland.
    Going to a high school coaching job from a college head coaching position was not as unusual as it might seem. These two high schools, fairly new at the time, were the largest in this region. The sports competition between them was fierce, especially in football.
    Annual football games between the two were played on Thanksgiving Day, often at Shields-Watkins Field, and the crowds numbered as high as 25,000, which in the early years was more than the number attending UT games.
    After the games, fan of the two schools made their way downtown, cheering and generally whooping it up for several hours. Fistfights often broke out.
    I graduated from KHS in 1950 and remember being in the throngs that jammed the sidewalks and streets at the corner of Gay Street and Clinch Avenue, a popular downtown corner.
    The competition ended in 1950 with the closing of KHS the following spring.
    Alums still argue over which high school was dominant in football. In the 39 years of competition, KHS won 21 times, CHS 14 times, and there were four ties, according to "The Alpha & Omega of the Trojan Dynasty," a history of KHS by Bill Hunt.
