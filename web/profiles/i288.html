    Obituary
    Born November 15, 1910, in Baltimore, Md.; died of a stroke, January 27 (one source says January 28), 1990, while traveling from London, England, to New York, N.Y. Journalist, editor, and novelist. Iams, who was known professionally as Jack Iams, began his career as a journalist for British and American newspapers. Iams had also been an editor for Atlas magazine, now called World Press Review. Praised for their wit and entertainment value, Iams's thirteen books include eight crime novels, of which Death Draws the Line is considered one of the best. He featured character Rocky Rockwell in a mystery trilogy consisting of Do Not Murder Before Christmas, A Shot of Murder, and What Rhymes With Murder? Iams's other works include Nowhere With Music, Table for Four, and Prematurely Gay.

    DeathJan 27, 1990New York City (All Boroughs), New York, United States
    Jack Iams, Novelist, Editor of Magazine And TV Critic, 79
    Published: January 29, 1990
    Jack Iams, a journalist, a novelist and an editor, died of a stroke on Saturday. At the time of his death, he and his wife, Joan Walker Wenning Iams, were en route to New York City from London. Mr. Iams, whose journalistic career spanned more than half a century, was 79 years old.

    At various times, he was a foreign correspondent, a writer for Newsweek, and an author of comic and mystery novels.

    Samuel Harvey Iams Jr. was born in Baltimore on Nov. 15, 1910. After graduating from St. Paul's School and Princeton University in 1932, he became a reporter for The London Daily Mail. He then wrote for other newspapers - including The Daily News - and Newsweek. He became a television critic for The New York Herald Tribune and was an editor of Atlas magazine, which is now known as World Press Review.

    'Brilliantly Witty'

    He wrote 13 books, including ''The Countess to Boot,'' ''Propelled by Experience'' and ''Do Not Murder Before Christmas.'' A review in The New York Times Book Review said his 1939 novel, ''Table for Four,'' was ''as brilliantly witty as the best of the British brand,'' and favorably compared it to Evelyn Waugh's ''Vile Bodies.''

    At Princeton, he was active in theatricals at the Triangle Club, along with James Stewart, Joshua Logan and Jose Ferrer. He was a member of the Players.

    Eighteen years ago, he retired to Opio in the South of France, where he continued to write. Having sold their house, he and his wife were returning to the United States.

    He is survived by two sons and a daughter by his first marriage. His son David is the society reporter of The Philadelphia Inquirer; John is a correspondent in Moscow for The Associated Press, and Alice Kittredge lives in Panama. There are four grandchildren.

    Obituary: Samuel Harvey Iams, Jr.
    1928 -Samuel Harvey lams, Jr. died of a stroke on January 29, 1990, while en route from London to New York City. The son of Elizabeth Rouse lams and Samuel Harvey lams, he was born in Baltimore, Maryland, on November 15, 1910, and entered the IV Form in 1925. As a VI Former he was a Supervisor; a Councillor; secretary of the Library Association; art editor of the Program Committee; a member of the executive committees of the Missionary Society and the Concordian Literary Society; a member of the board of Horae Scholasticae; and a member of the Dramatic Club and the Mandolin Club. He received his diploma .magna cum laude.

    After graduating from Princeton in 1932, where he was active in the Triangle Club, he began his journalistic careeer with The London Daily Mail; he wrote also for The Daily News, The New York Herald Tribune, Newsweek, and Atlas magazine. During World War II he was the bureau chief in French Equatorial Africa, Belgian Congo, Lisbon, and Brussels for the Office of War Information between May 1942 and December 1945.

    He was the author of 13 books, mostly mysteries. For the past 18 years he lived in Opio, France, and was moving back to the United States at the time of his death.
