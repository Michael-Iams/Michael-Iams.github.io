    Man carrying $149K attacked by gunman in wild midtown heist outside Starbucks

    BY KERRY BURKE , ALISON GENDAR AND LARRY MCSHANE / DAILY NEWS STAFF WRITERS

    SATURDAY, FEBRUARY 23, 2008, 1:50 AM


    Read more: http://www.nydailynews.com/news/crime/man-carrying-149k-attacked-gunman-wild-midtown-heist-starbucks-article-1.308402#ixzz2WTBjQUCV


    A brazen gunman escaped with $149,000 in cash Friday after beating a music executive bloody outside a midtown Starbucks as stunned mocha-sippers watched in disbelief.
    The gunman stalked Seton Ijams from the lobby of a nearby bank after watching him make the large withdrawal and then jumped him outside the coffee house, police sources said.
    A battered Ijams - yelling "Please help me!" - was dragged by the gunman along the slushy sidewalk in a violent struggle for the cash, witnesses said.
    The bandit pistol-whipped Ijams, beat him and fired one shot, possibly grazing Ijams, who then surrendered a black sports bag stuffed with cash, witnesses and police said.
    The black-clad gunman - looking carefully to his left and right - disappeared east toward Sixth Ave. before slack-jawed witnesses on W. 56th St.
    "Nobody screamed," said limo driver Zafer Incekara, who called 911. "We were all in shock.
    "I've never seen anything like it before."
    Messenger Jose Hernandez said he was nearly shot when he sprang to Ijams' aid outside the Starbucks.
    "The guy was getting pistol-whipped for his bag, but he wouldn't let go," said Hernandez, 23, of Queens. "I went to help, but the [robber] cocked back his gun."
    It was unclear why Ijams, a 49-year-old executive with Columbia Artists Management Inc. on Broadway, had taken such a large amount of money out of the bank, police said.
    His face covered in blood, Ijams was taken to New York-Presbyterian Hospital Weill Cornell where he was in stable condition with a head wound.
    A horde of detectives questioned him in the emergency room as doctors wrapped his head in bandages.
    Cops believe much of the violence was captured by video cameras in the bank and at buildings along 56th St., including the Le Parker Meridien - a ritzy hotel where rooms with a Central Park view go for at least $600 a night.
    Ijams withdrew the cash from a Chase branch on Sixth Ave. shortly before 2 p.m., police sources said. He was carrying the money inside the sports bag when the attacker jumped him.
    Incekara, of Lyndhurst, N.J., was driving his limo down the block when he saw the two men wrestling over the bag.
    The driver, his passengers and people on the street watched as the pair battled violently, with a bleeding Ijams getting the worst of it.
    "His head was open. It was bleeding," the limo driver said.
    Hernandez said it appeared the assailant's handgun fired accidentally when he tripped and fell. Hernandez said he watched as the gunman, who remained on the loose Friday night, escaped with the cash. He was described as a black male dressed in a black hooded jacket and black pants.
    Ijams' job puts him in contact with a variety of international music acts, including the Djoliba Ensemble of Mali and the State Symphony Orchestra of Mexico.
    Doormen at his upper East Side co-op described him as an easygoing Mets fan who sometimes donned a tuxedo for music industry events.
    He also is treasurer for the St. Nicholas Society of the City of New York - an organization founded in 1835 by Washington Irving. To join, members must have a relative who lived in New York City before 1785. Ijams is a collateral descendant of Mother Elizabeth Bayley Seton, the first native-born American to become a saint.

    ANATOMY OF A PERFECT HEIST
    MYSTERY OF MIDTOWN BASH & GRAB

    By BRAD HAMILTON and PHILIP MESSSING
    Last Updated: 3:43 AM, March 9, 2008
    Posted: 3:43 AM, March 9, 2008
    It might have been the perfect heist.

    Two weeks after a music executive was robbed of almost $150,000 cash on a Midtown sidewalk in broad daylight, the victim has cooperated with police, 50 interviews have been conducted and surveillance tape of the action has been scrutinized - but there are no suspects.

    Cops say they believe the victim is "on the up-and-up." Other observers say the whole thing stinks of an inside job.

    Seton Ijams had called ahead to his Chase bank branch, at 56th Street and Sixth Avenue, on Feb. 22, telling them he would be arriving shortly to withdraw $149,000 in cash.

    It was a familiar routine. He left his office at Broadway and 57th Street, reaching Chase just before 2 p.m.

    The money was waiting for him on the second floor, and he had a black duffel bag in which to carry it.

    He took his regular route back, down a long escalator to the bank's first floor, out onto 56th Street and across Sixth Avenue.

    Ijams reached the west side of the avenue and passed in front of a crowded Starbucks, heading west toward his office at Columbia Artists Management.

    At that moment, a man in a dark hooded jacket approached him, pulled a handgun and grabbed the bag. Ijams, 50, refused to give up the duffel and hollered for help.

    The man dragged Ijams and smashed him in the forehead with the gun. He dropped the gun and a shot went off, hitting no one.

    With blood gushing from Ijams' wound, the thief ripped the bag away and disappeared, heading east on 56th Street.

    The bundles of $100, $50 and $20 bills were payments for Columbia's classical-musical acts, which he manages as company vice president, Ijams told police.

    Just three days later, the label's biggest draw, the New York Philharmonic, was set to depart for a concert in North Korea, but police could not say if the cash was intended for them.

    Sporting stitches over his left eye and a red mark over his right, Ijams emerged last week from his East 71st Street co-op.

    "He hasn't been back to work, yet, but I think he's doing OK," said neighbor Marcia Schecter.

    His brother, Henry, and pals are helping him get back on his feet.

    Ijams' battle has earned him the admiration of his blueblood pals in the St. Nicholas Society of the City of New York, an association of those whose ancestors arrived in the city prior to 1785. Ironically, Ijams, a member since 1990, is the society's treasurer and a descendant of William Seton, Alexander Hamilton's cashier at the Bank of New York.
