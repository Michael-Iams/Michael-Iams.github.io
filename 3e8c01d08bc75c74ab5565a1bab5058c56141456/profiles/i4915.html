    Note #1

    http://www2.statesville.com/news/2011/jan/29/vietnam-veterans-disillus ionment-slowly-turns-prid-ar-737930/

    By: DONNA SWICEGOOD | Statesville Record & Landmark
    Published: January 29, 2011

    When Leon Ijames landed back in the United States after 13 months in Vietnam, he was greeted by shouts of “baby killer” with a racial slur thrown in as well.
    That experience left the 22-year-old bitter and angry.
    “I’d just spent 13 months in a war zone, defending these people,” he said. “I was extremely bitter."
    Back home in North Carolina, his father changed that bitterness to acceptance and, finally, to pride. “My dad just kept talking to me,” he said.
    Ijames, once disillusioned about his time serving his country, is now proud to be a veteran. He’s active in veteran’s organizations and he’s especially proud to call himself a Vietnam veteran.
    When that draft notice came in 1966, Ijames said, he wasn’t surprised. “I fully expected it,” he said. “The conflict was really raging over there (Vietnam).”
    He initially considered going to Canada to dodge the draft - like many others did in that era. His father nixed that idea.
    Instead, he reported for basic training. A few months later, he was in Saigon. “I was scared to death,” he said.
    Initially assigned as a clerk - because he could type -- Ijames eventually was assigned to set up communications equipment. He traveled throughout the southern part of Vietnam, setting up equipment and dodging firefights.
    One perk of his assignment was his ability to call home more often than most soldiers. “My parents had to pay for it, but I did get to call home three or four times,” he said.
    Ijames said he was offered the chance to get out of the Army earlier for an extra month or so in Vietnam. He turned that down. He remembered the last sight he saw as he shipped out to Vietnam. “I never forgot the sight of my dad standing there with tears streaming down his face. He didn’t think he’d ever see me alive again,” Ijames said.
    After returning from Vietnam, he served out the remainder of his tour of duty in Fort Sill, Okla., where he faced the possibility of dealing with the race riots that were raging in some large cities.
    “I refused to do that. I’m not going to take up a rifle and bayonet against my fellow Americans," he said.
    Like most veterans serving in war zones, Ijames missed some of the simple comforts of home. A hot shower in a private environment was one thing Ijames said he enjoyed when he got the opportunity during leave in Sydney, Australia.
    Once back in the states, he said, his mother’s cornbread was especially delicious.
    Even though he was back in the U.S., at his parents’ home, at times, Ijames said, he was still back in Vietnam.
    He remembered one time when he was napping on the couch at his parents’ house. “My dad came in and he grabbed me. I decked him,” he said. His year in a war zone made him hypersensitive.
    Ijames said he was also sensitive about the issue of race. After fighting for his country, he returned to racial slurs and bias. He talked about picking up a new car, one he’d ordered while overseas, and driving home in it. “I had a patrolman accuse me of stealing that car,” he said. “That added to my bitterness."
    His father was a minister, however, and he understood, on some level, what his son was experiencing. “He was used to listening to what you had to say,” Ijames said.
    That outlet proved to be what Ijames needed to recover.
    He went back to school, worked for his father’s janitorial business and worked a second job at night, and he put Vietnam behind him.
    Like many Vietnam veterans, he avoided telling people he was a vet. “Up until the early 1990s I didn’t say anything about being a Vietnam vet,” he said.
    It was during that time period that he learned a friend and someone he’d known most of his life, Skip McCall, was also a Vietnam vet.
    Now, he said, the recognition that Vietnam vets never got when they returned from overseas is coming their way and it’s long overdue.
    Coming to terms with his 13 months in Vietnam also helped him gain pride in his service to his country.
    He’s now a proponent of veteran’s issues, whether serving as the past commander (the first African-American commander) of VFW Post 2031, chairperson of the Iredell County Veterans Council, quartermaster of the VFW District 14 for North Carolina, or simply as a member of American Legion Post 217.
    Ijames is also active in civic organizations as a member of the Fourth Creek Rotary Club.
    Ijames now unashamedly tells everyone about his service in Vietnam.
    “I’m glad I did it,” he said. “I don’t regret having service for this country. I’m proud of it.“
