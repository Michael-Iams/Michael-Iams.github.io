Few families have played a larger role than the Mulhollands in the development of Los Angeles and the San Fernando Valley. Catherine Mulholland's grandfather, William Mulholland, was Chief Superintendent of the Los Angeles Bureau of Water Works and Supply (now the Department of Water and Power.) As such, he built the Los Angeles Aqueduct, which to this day brings water from the Sierra Nevada Mountains and Owens Valley to the San Fernando Valley and greater Los Angeles.
William's eldest son and Catherine Mulholland’s father, Perry, established and managed a 640-acre ranch for himself and his four siblings on land in the San Fernando Valley. Known as the Mulholland Orchard Company, the ranch remained in operation until 1965 and encompassed large portions of what are now the communities of Northridge and Canoga Park.
Catherine Mulholland loved libraries and the Oviatt in particular, and donated her personal archives and those of her family to the Library in order to preserve this important history for future generations. Her remarkable collection, dating back to the 1860s, is a journey through Valley history as seen through photographs, scrapbooks, memorabilia, clothing and ranch records of this pioneer family.

Includes this text:

The Reverend Benjamin Albert Imes (b. Jan. 28, 1848, d. Aug. 5, 1908) was born to free Black parents near Mercersburg, Pennsylvania. He graduated from Oberlin College in 1877 and Oberlin Theological Seminary in 1881, having already (Nov. 1880) been ordained to the ministry at Memphis, becoming the first Black pastor of the Second Congregational Church in Memphis, which he served until 1892. Ida B. Wells attended Second Congregational at that time and was inspired by his sermons combining advanced education with religious fervor.
 
From 1892 to 1895 he was minister of a church in Knoxville and during the following year financial agent of Berea college. In 1896 he went to Normal, Alabama as chaplain and Instructor in the American Missionary Association college there. During 1900-02 he was pastor and public school principal at Brewton, Alabama; preached in Mobile during 1902-04 and taught in the Emerson institute; preached at Beloit, Alabama 1905-06 and was in charge of the U.P. mission school at Prairie, Alabama from 1906 until his death. He died at his home in Prairie of bronchitis August 5 1908.
 
His wife Elizabeth Rachel Wallace Imes (b. 1852, d. 1944) had been born in slavery in Natchez, Mississippi and also attended Oberlin where she had been brought as a child.
 
One of their sons, Elmer Samuel Imes, became a renowned physicist and the second African American to earn a Ph.D. in Physics (and the first in the 20th century): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elmer_Imes
 
A Tennessee historical marker about Rev. Imes is at the Second Congregational Church building on Walker. It reads: "Described as the best-educated minister in all of Memphis in 1880 was a black man, the Rev. Benjamin A. Imes, who was a noted city leader. Imes held two degrees from Oberlin College and was involved with an influential group that pushed for the integrations of public facilities in Memphis during the late 19th century. The group met with its demise during the race riots of the 1880s. Imes was minister of Second Congregational Church, founded in 1868."
